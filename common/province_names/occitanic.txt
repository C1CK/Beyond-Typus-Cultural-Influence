#A NOTE ON SPELLING
#OCCITAN - Generally 'OA' and not 'UA'. 'What' wants to be 'Oat' and not 'Uat', which Catalan may (potentially) like more. Places named after a saint typically have the word 'saint' and their name as separate words. Trasnliterations with -ook/-uk/-uc should be '-uc'. Save the '-òc' for the long ò.
#Occitan sometimes loses the -ium and -is in toponyms along with Piedmontese, where Italian would keep them as -i or -io. Elysium -> Aliscamps (Cognate:'Alis'); Italian 'Campi Alisi'.
#However, these -ius is kept in the name of saints. Bausèli, Salvi, Antòni, Jòrdi...
#French -ll- generally becomes -l-, unless it's making a Y sound, in which case -lh-.
#
#Sent and Sant coexist in Occitan; neither has a universal majority. Gascon can have Sent and Provençal have Sant, but the 'Occitain' and 'Auvergnat' cultures in BT are pretty split.
#^As such, I'm using both based on either etymology or what the nearest dialect uses. N France gets 'Sent' partly due to Limousin, Marchois and Poitevin-Saintongeais, partly because that's the closest shift to what French has RL.
#^Navarra gets 'Sent' as Gascon has native names for the cities here, using 'Sent'.
#^Italy and Arpitania, likewise, get 'Sant' as that's the nearest dialectally and linguistically (Vivaroalpine).
#^Catalonia, closest sister to Occitan, uses 'Sant' just as in Languedoc, so that's what she gets. Extending 'Sant' to the rest of Iberia, as that's closest to local (plus potentially Occitanoromance Aragonese has 'Sant').
#&TLDR:Sant - Romance-speaking Iberia, Italy, Arpitania, Mediterranean. Sent - N France, British Isles, possibly Americas (most likely to be settled from Atlantic-facing territories - ie Gascony&Poitou - as hisotrical).
#
#SPANISH NEW-WORLD TERMS
#CAYO (m) - French CAYE (f), English CAY. Here I'm using "Cai", as "Càia" would sound like "Calla", which is "shut up" in Catalan. :P
#
#ENGLISH Toponyms
#'Caster' is 'Castria' in Latin, which has an Occitan evolution in toponymy:Càstria. Using this.
#'Chester' comes from Latin 'Castrum', which is 'Castre' in Occitan despite not being used much.
#'Bury' comes from 'burg'. While Norman likes 'Béry' for these, Occitan is far south enough that certain word-ending changes to Y from consonants never happened...I'm more confident using Occitan 'borg' than trying to craft from French forms.
